# CHCC Value Proposition: The Investment Case
## Why This Project Will Be Profitable — and How We Repay Every Dollar

---

## The Core Argument in One Paragraph

The Charlotte Hindu Community Center is not a charity that needs perpetual donations to survive. It is a **revenue-generating civic institution** backed by a $2 million capital campaign and designed to reach operational self-sufficiency by Year 2 and full profitability by Year 3. Every dollar raised in Phase 1 funds infrastructure that directly generates recurring earned revenue — school tuition, wellness memberships, food programs, event rentals — across 9 distinct income streams. Community loan donors will be repaid in full. The center will carry no debt by Year 5 and is projected to generate a $1.7M+ annual surplus by Year 3.

---

## Part 1: Why South Charlotte?

### A Market with No Competitor

**South Charlotte** — spanning Ballantyne, Pineville, Matthews, Marvin, Waxhaw, and Indian Trail — is the epicenter of Indian-American settlement in the Carolinas.

| Metric | Data |
|--------|------|
| Indian-Americans in Greater Charlotte | 80,000+ |
| Indian-Americans in South Charlotte Corridor | ~40,000 (est.) |
| Median Household Income (South Charlotte) | $140,000–$175,000 |
| % with advanced degrees (Indian-American households) | 72% |
| Dominant professions | IT, Medicine, Finance, Entrepreneurship |
| Annual household charitable giving (est.) | $3,000–$12,000 per family |
| Existing dedicated Hindu community center in Charlotte | **Zero** |

**The competitive gap is total.** There is no purpose-built, multi-program Hindu community center in all of Mecklenburg, Union, Cabarrus, or Gaston counties. The community currently fragments across rented church halls, hotel ballrooms, and private homes. Every dollar of wallet share currently goes to facilities with no connection to community values.

### Why South Charlotte Wins on Every Metric

- **Density:** The Ballantyne–Matthews corridor has the highest concentration of Indian households per square mile in the Carolinas.
- **Disposable income:** South Charlotte Indian-American households have the purchasing power to sustain premium-priced wellness, education, and cultural programming.
- **School-age children:** The area has a high proportion of families with children aged 5–18 — the exact demographic for Aum School, heritage arts, and youth sports.
- **Professional class:** Physicians, attorneys, tech executives, and business owners — the primary audience for the Community Loan Program — are disproportionately concentrated here.
- **Zero alternatives:** There is no yoga studio teaching authentic Ashtanga in a Hindu cultural context, no Bharatanatyam academy, no Ayurvedic clinic, no Gaushala — anywhere in Charlotte.

## Part 2: The Revenue Model — 9 Streams, Zero Dependence on Donations by Year 3

The fundamental insight: **CHCC is a service business, not a charity.** The fundraising campaign funds the infrastructure. The infrastructure generates revenue. The revenue funds operations, repays loans, and builds endowment. Donations become a growth accelerant, not a survival mechanism.

### Revenue Projections by Stream

| Revenue Stream | Year 1 | Year 2 | Year 3 |
|----------------|--------|--------|--------|
| Aum School Tuition (50 → 100 → 200 students) | $400,000 | $900,000 | $1,600,000 |
| Yoga & Wellness Memberships + Drop-In | $120,000 | $220,000 | $350,000 |
| Ayurvedic Treatments (clinic) | $60,000 | $110,000 | $180,000 |
| Heritage Arts Programs (dance, music, martial arts) | $80,000 | $145,000 | $220,000 |
| Gaushala Programs (Meet & Feed, sponsorships, A2 CSA) | $45,000 | $100,000 | $180,000 |
| Sattvic Food Court & Juice Bar | $90,000 | $180,000 | $300,000 |
| Vedic Retail Store | $30,000 | $65,000 | $120,000 |
| Event Rentals (auditorium, studios, halls) | $40,000 | $90,000 | $150,000 |
| Corporate Wellness Partnerships | $25,000 | $60,000 | $100,000 |
| **Total Earned Revenue** | **$890,000** | **$1,870,000** | **$3,200,000** |

### Why These Numbers Are Conservative

- **Aum School:** $8,000/student/year tuition (well below private school average of $14,000–$22,000 in South Charlotte). 200 students = $1.6M. This assumes no waitlist, no scholarship displacement, and 2-year ramp.
- **Yoga Memberships:** Priced at $80/month — below Lifetime Fitness ($100+) and boutique studios ($150+). 365 members = $350K/year.
- **Gaushala:** S3 Gaushala (Memphis, TN) generates $180K+/year from 15 cows and 3 programs. CHCC models conservatively against their proven benchmark.
- **Food Court:** 100 covers/day × $9 average × 330 operating days = $297,000. Conservative for a captive audience community center.

## Part 4: Loan Repayment Schedule — Every Dollar Returned

### Community Loan Commitments

CHCC's Community Loan Program offers three structures across $50,000 / $100,000 / $150,000 amounts. Here is the projected repayment trajectory assuming $500,000 in Vriddhi (4% interest-deferred) loans and $300,000 in Nishkama (0% interest-free) loans are raised in Phase 1:

| Obligation | Amount | Due By | Funded From |
|-----------|--------|--------|-------------|
| Nishkama principal (interest-free) | $300,000 | Year 3–5 (donor's choice) | Year 2–3 surplus |
| Vriddhi principal + 4% interest | $500,000 + $100,000 | Year 3–7 (balloon at term) | Year 3 surplus / reserves |
| Moksha Daan forgiveness (already donated) | N/A | Annual tranches | No repayment required |
| **Total Loan Obligation (worst case)** | **$900,000** | By Year 7 | Covered by $2.5M cumulative surplus (Year 3) |

### Repayment Confidence

The $2,502,000 cumulative surplus by end of Year 3 **exceeds all loan obligations by 2.8×** — even under conservative revenue assumptions. CHCC can repay every dollar lent without touching the capital campaign funds or endowment.

**Stress Test (30% Revenue Shortfall Scenario):**
Even if revenue comes in 30% below projections across all three years:
- Year 3 total revenue: $2,240,000 (vs. projected $3.2M)
- Year 3 net surplus: $775,000
- Cumulative 3-year surplus: ~$900,000
- Still sufficient to repay Nishkama loans in full; Vriddhi repaid by Year 5.

## Part 6: Why This Is a Safe Investment

### Risk Mitigation

| Risk | Likelihood | Mitigation |
|------|-----------|------------|
| Aum School enrollment below target | Low | 40,000+ Indian families in South Charlotte; waitlist expected within 6 months |
| Wellness revenue below projection | Low | Pre-launch membership drive; founding member pricing locks in 200+ members |
| Facility cost overrun | Medium | 6-month operating reserve ($120,000) in capital budget; phased build-out |
| Key staff departure | Low | Competitive salaries; mission-driven hiring; 3-year employment contracts for principals |
| Economic downturn reducing memberships | Medium | Diverse revenue (9 streams); Aum School tuition is "sticky" — parents don't withdraw mid-year |
| CHCC unable to repay loans | Very Low | $2.5M cumulative surplus by Year 3 covers $900K max loan obligation 2.8× |

### Board Accountability
- Finance Committee reviews all expenditures over $5,000
- Annual CPA audit; results published on website within 90 days of fiscal year end
- All community loan repayments governed by signed pledge agreements — legally binding
- Vriddhi ($100K+) lenders receive quarterly financial reports and Advisory Council seats
